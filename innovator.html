Innovator Information

Brief Background

 Dr. Shirley Jackson was born on August 5th, 1946. During her childhood, she took a particular interest in science. Pursuing her interest, she would go on to be the first Black woman to earn a PhD in physics from MIT.
 From there, she earned a position at Bell Laboratories, and in 1995, she was appointed the head of the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission, becoming a leading expert in theoretical physics. Eventually, “she became the eighteenth president of Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute” (The HistoryMakers, 2006).



Underrepresentation

 At MIT, Dr. Shirley Jackson was “among the first African American students to attend MIT, and in her undergraduate class, she was one of only two women” (The HistoryMakers, 2006). To follow that up, she would go on to become the first woman to earn a PhD in physics from MIT.
 Beyond these achievements, it's important to note she excelled in an environment that was institutionally hostile against her. She was forced to attend segregated schools growing up and was once even told by her school advisor that “colored girls should learn a trade” (Success Academy, 2019). Considering that Dr. Shirley Jackson became the head of the U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission, principal of the Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, co-chair of the President’s Intelligence Advisory Board, and a recipient of the National Medal of Science, it is safe to say she has overcome the institutional prejudice against her and was better staying out of trade school.



Contributions

 Early research by Dr. Shirley Jackson in telecommunication has enabled the development of modern forms of communication. While working at Bell Laboratories, “her research there contributed to the inventions of the touch-tone telephone, fiber-optic cables, caller ID, and call waiting” (Tara Ramanathan, 2023).
 These massive innovations are the groundwork for the development of future technologies, such as the advancement to 6G networks, which enable high-speed global communication, virtual reality, and smart city networks. Her physics research has helped develop the growing field of quantum communication, which provides ultra-secure transmission of information.



Reason for Selection

 Our innovator selection decision was between Hedy Lamarr and Dr. Shirley Jackson. Both are innovators who experienced underrepresentation and overcame the odds to be incredibly influential on technological development. Hedy Lamarr helped invent a frequency-hopping signal during World War 2 that prevents torpedoes from being jammed or tracked. Her contributions are still used in Bluetooth and Wi-Fi; however, we decided against her being our innovator because Hedy Lamarr is fairly well known, whereas Dr. Shirley Jackson was truly underrepresented.
 Dr. Shirley Jackson’s ability as a trailblazer, groundbreaking achievements, and perseverance over hardship are ones to admire, and her contributions are, in our opinion, more substantial and important than most people realize. As modern technology continues to advance, the legacy of Dr. Shirley Jackson will continue to stand the test of time as a crucial stepping stone in technological development.
